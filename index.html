Growing up as Boys and Girls

1. Society shapes the perceptions of acceptable behavior and roles for boys and girls, but these expectations can vary across different societies.

2. In many societies, men and women are not valued equally, and this inequality often becomes evident in the division of labor and roles they perform.

Growing up in Samoa in the 1920s

1. In 1920s Samoa, children didn't attend school but learned important life skills from older children and adults, including childcare and household work.

2. Fishing was a significant activity, and young people were trained for long fishing expeditions, but the timing of this training varied during childhood.

3. Boys started outdoor tasks like fishing and planting coconuts around the age of nine, while girls continued childcare and errands until their teenage years.

4. Teenage girls gained more freedom, participated in fishing trips, worked in plantations, and acquired skills like weaving and cooking.

Growing up Male in Madhya Pradesh in the 1960s

1. In the 1960s in a small town in Madhya Pradesh, boys and girls attended separate schools from Class VI onwards.

2. The girls' school was designed with a central courtyard that provided seclusion and safety for their playtime, whereas the boys' school had no such feature, and their playground was an open space attached to the school.

3. After school, the boys often observed hundreds of school girls walking through the narrow streets. These girls appeared purposeful in contrast to the boys, who used the streets for idling, playing, and trying bicycle tricks.

4. The girls walked in groups, possibly due to concerns about teasing or potential attacks on the streets.

5. The street served as a direct route for girls to get home, emphasizing their need for safety and efficiency.

6. This account highlights the distinct experiences and perceptions of boys and girls as they navigated their daily lives in the town.

Valuing Household Work

1. Jaspreet's family, like many others, did not consider housework as genuine work, a sentiment that is not unique to them.

2. Globally, the primary responsibility for housework and caregiving, which includes taking care of family members like children, the elderly, and the sick, typically falls on women.

3. However, this work done by women within the home is often unrecognized and undervalued by society. It's assumed to be a natural duty and not deserving of payment, leading to the devaluation of this essential work.

Lives of Domestic Workers

1. Domestic workers, predominantly women, play a crucial role in many households, particularly in urban areas, performing tasks such as cleaning, washing, cooking, and taking care of family members.

2. Domestic work is often undervalued, leading to low wages for these workers. Their workdays can be extremely long, starting as early as 5 in the morning and ending as late as 12 at night.

3. Despite their hard work, domestic workers frequently do not receive the respect they deserve from their employers, as many employers fail to acknowledge their contributions.

4. Melani, a domestic worker in Delhi, shared her experience of working for a rich family, where she faced long hours, limited food, and a lack of respect from her employers, highlighting the challenges faced by domestic workers.

Physical Demands and Time-Consuming Nature of Housework

1. The undervaluing of women's household and care-giving work is not limited to individual families but is part of a broader system of gender inequality.

2. Addressing this issue requires action at both individual and government levels, aligning with the principles of equality in the Constitution.

3. The Constitution emphasizes that discrimination based on gender should not occur, despite the existence of gender inequalities.

4. The government is dedicated to understanding the causes of gender inequality and implementing measures to address the issue.

5. One recognized aspect is that women and girls often bear the burden of child-care and housework, indicating the need for steps to rectify this inequality.
